Introduction

This section is where I've compiled a list of transliteration attempts I've made for English. Note that some scripts are more suited for English phonologyThe sounds of a language and how they work. Includes aspects such as phonemes (sounds that distinguish meaning) and phonotactics (a language's syllable structure). than others, so some imagination should be used to see how I came up with these transliteration systems (and some, such as Shavian are specifically designed for English). Also, further transliterations back into the Latin script will be given below each in orange so you can follow the symbols being used if you're unfamiliar with a certain script. Assume the writing direction is left-to-right unless stated otherwise with the appropriate arrow (right-to-left being indicated by ← and top-to-bottom with ↓; the orange romanizations will always be read left-to-right).

Note that there is inconsistency in the various romanizations because I wanted to follow each script's romanization standards as much as I could (although many scripts have more than one romanization method, so I chose what I felt were the most relevant). Additionally, some scripts used here do not entirely adhere to native rules of punctuation so as to aid the reader in following along with each word used (for example, Burmese and Thai do not add spaces between every word when used for the languages they were designed for, but they do here for English).

Finally, also note that some entries are images and not text. This is because some scripts have minimal or no Unicode support, so the fonts can't always be "pasted in." Credit goes to Keyman and Lexilogos for permitting many of these writing systems to be easily typed out.

The sentence is "the quick brown fox jumps over the lazy dog" and the phonemicThe term phonemic refers to representing the English sentence by its distinctive sounds (or phonemes). None of the transliterations I'm using are based on just switching one letter for another as though it were a cypher. For example, the word "quick" will not be transliterated into Neo-Tifinagh as ⵇⵓⵉⵛⴽ (this spelling being the Neo-Tifinagh analogues the letters q, u, i, c and k), but rather as ⴽⵡⵉⴽ (this spelling phonemically transcribing the English word into Neo-Tifinagh as k, w, i and k) Therefore, in this case, we treat "quick" as though it were spelled kwik, as this better represents the sounds and ignores unneeded symbols (the letters q, u and c in "quick" are redundant; notice you'll still pronounce it the same if it were just kwik). transcription I'm basing the transliterations off of is /ðiː kwɪk bɹaʊn fɒks dʒʌmps oʊvəɹ ðiː leɪzɪ dɒg/.
