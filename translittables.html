Tibetan script for English

ཏི་བཻ་ཏན་སྐྲིཔྟ་ཕཽར་ཨིང་གླིཤ​​

Preliminaries:

  • Word spacing is only used after punctuation marks like the shad ། (the Tibetan equivalent of a period), question mark, etc. A space by itself is the equivalent of a comma.
  • Each syllable is followed by the tsheg ་. For example, the word "computer" would be spelled ཀམ་པྱཱུ་ཏར​ (kam-pyū-tar). The last syllable is followed by a tsheg if another syllable follows.
  • When a word begins with a vowel, the letter ཨ​ (by itself conveying the sound /ə/) must be used to "carry" the vowels. For example, "international" is spelled ཨིན་ཏར་ནྱཱ་ཤ་ནལ (note the vowel being carried here). The only exception is the vowel འ.
  • Being an abugida, letters can have an inherent /ə/ sound when they are not syllable-final and no letter within a syllable has a visible vowel marker.
  • Consonants clusters are often indicated by writing the following consonants as subscripts below the first of the cluster. Theoretically, however, these never need to be used (which can be useful since some devices do not support Tibetan as well as others).

Phoenician script for English

𐤐𐤍𐤉𐤔𐤍 𐤎𐤊𐤓𐤐𐤕 𐤐𐤅𐤓 𐤀𐤏𐤂𐤋𐤔

Preliminaries:

  • Some vowels (listed with ∅ in the tables) are not marked at all and must be known beforehand. Diacritcal marks for the vowels did not come until later in history.
  • Some letters have more than one pronunciation and must be known beforehand. As with vowels, affricated consonants (like p becoming ph) and the specific vocalic values of matres lectionis were not indicated until later.
  • Writing direction is right-to-left.
  • Any syllable that begins with a vowel must be marked with the aleph 𐤀 before the vowel; this includes the vowels that are already indicated by the aleph, such as /ɒ/ and /ɑ:/.
