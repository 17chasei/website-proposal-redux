What is an abjad?

An abjad is a type of writing system that "prioritizes" consonantal letters over vocalic (relating to vowels) letters. In other words, consonants are always written (like an alphabet), but vowels are often omitted, with certain exceptions. The word comes from a combination of the first four letters of the Arabic script (alif ا, baa ب, jeem ج and daal د). To demonstrate how an abjad works, consider this example showing what English might look like if it was written in an abjad style: hllo and wlcm t th wrttn wrld"Hello and welcome to The Written World.".

One unfamiliar with abjads might wonder why writing systems that don't explicitly mark vowels would be used, considering that it could potentially leave the reader "guessing" what vowel goes where, if any. However, this does not usually prove to be a problem for readers for a few reasons:

  • In scripts such as Arabic and Hebrew, it's generally the case that dictionaries, texts for learners, and holy scriptures will mark all the vowels. So in this case, those who either grow up learning an abjad or learn it later eventually become familiar with reading without vowel markings (like "training wheels" for writing).
  • Some languages that use abjads, such as Hebrew, Arabic, and Aramaic have grammatical systems that make "guessing" the vowels much easier than many other langauges would allow. These languages (called the Semitic languages) rely upon a word-building system of triconsonantal roots (word roots that have three consonants, but not always three; e.g. the root for "king" in Semitic is M-L-K or some variation). Within these consonantal roots, vowels are inserted into various spaces between consonants, which can form words. For example, the Hebrew root S-F-R has a general meaning of "writing," but filling in this root with the right vowels forms actual words (thus, sefer (ספר) means "book" and sofer (סופר) means "scribe").
  • If you could read the vowel-purged English sentence above, you've demonstrated that abjads are not necessarily inefficient writing systems, even for non-Semitic languages like English. In fact, a lot of non-Semitic languages use abjads, such as Farsi, Pashto, and Urdu (all of which are related to English).

I wrote earlier that there are certain exceptions to when vowels are written. Usually, abjads explicitly write what are known as "long vowels." Long vowels are simply variants of a vowel that are pronounced longer than their "short" counterparts (the short vowels being what are often omitted). However, some "long vowels" in certain languages (like modern Hebrew) aren't actually long at all. For example, in the Hebrew word sofer סופר, the o-vowel (represented here by the Hebrew letter vav ו) is explicitly written, but the e-vowel is not. Why? Because historically, the o-vowel would have been distinguished from its short counterpart and the e-vowel was short, but Hebrew no longer distinguishes vowel length, so sometimes the o-vowel is written despite not being long.The o-vowel isn't always marked by the vav. The Hebrew name Moshe משה (equivalent of the name Moses) does not have a vav for the o-sound. Also, while standard Hebrew does not make the distinction anymore, some dialects still do.

Historically, this wasn't always the case with abjads. The first ever abjad - and one of the first ever writing systems - the Egyptian hieroglyphs, did not indicate any vowels at all, and neither did its earlier descendants, such as Proto-Sinaitic and Phoenician. Furthermore, it should be noted that these vocalic letters, like the vav, originally only represented a consonant (or more specifically, a semivowel), but later began to also indicate (long) vowels that were pronounced in the same place in the mouth as the semivowel.In linguistics, this is called the place of articulation.

What are some examples of abjads?

  • Ancient abjads: Egyptian hieroglyphs, Phoenician, Ugaritic, Sogdian, Pahlavi
  • Modern abjads: Arabic, Hebrew, Syriac

What are some languages that use abjads?

  • Arabic script: Arabic, Farsi, Urdu, Pashto, Pakistani Punjabi, (sometimes) Malay, Iranian Azeri
  • Hebrew script: Hebrew, Aramaic, (in certain contexts) YiddishWhile Yiddish certainly does use the Hebrew script, its form is technically an alphabet, not an abjad. However, words borrowed from Hebrew are adopted wholesale, so abjad-like elements are still present., (some dialects) Aramaic
